# Stone Creek Church — Brand Voice Reference
**V 1.1 · 2025–2026**
 
---
 
## The Foundation: What Voice Serves
 
Stone Creek's voice exists to serve its mission — fostering environments where God transforms individuals. Every word either opens a door or closes one. The voice should feel like a trusted friend who takes faith seriously without taking themselves too seriously. Warm. Direct. Real.
 
---
 
## Who We're Writing For
 
Stone Creek's primary unreached audience is a person we call **"Sophisticated Sarah"** — a woman in her 30s–40s who is culturally engaged, intellectually curious, and spiritually skeptical of traditional church. She values:
- **Personal growth** — she reads Brené Brown and Simon Sinek, not devotionals
- **Cultural exploration** — she's drawn to novelty, aesthetics, and experience
- **Authentic community** — she craves belonging but is wary of anything that feels manufactured
- **Rest and balance** — she's stretched thin by work, parenting, and finances, and she knows it
- **Open dialogue** — she has real questions and won't engage with content that feels like it already has all the answers
She is often Christian-but-not-active, or spiritually curious but skeptical. She finds overtly "churchy" content **dull and disconnected from real life.** She won't respond to guilt or high-commitment asks. She will respond to language that meets her where she is — sophisticated, empathetic, and honest about the tension of modern life.
 
**Every caption, CTA, and content series should pass this gut-check:** *Would Sarah find this relevant to her actual life, or does it sound like something only an insider would care about?*
 
---
 
## The Four Communication Commitments
 
These come directly from the brand guide. They are not preferences — they are requirements.
 
### 1. Be Culturally Relevant
Speak into the world people actually live in. Reference real seasons, real pressures, real questions. Don't retreat to a Christian subculture bubble. The brand engages culture rather than commenting on it from a distance.
 
### 2. Be Tech Savvy
Use platforms natively. A caption is not a bulletin insert. A social post is not an announcement. Short-form content should feel built for the format it lives in, not repurposed from somewhere else.
 
### 3. Use "Everyday" Language
Write like a thoughtful person talking to a friend — not like a church bulletin, a theological treatise, or a press release. Avoid insider vocabulary unless it's explained. If you wouldn't say it out loud to a stranger at coffee, don't write it.
 
### 4. Be Customized for Its Audience
Main brand, Kids, and Youth (YTH) are distinct voices for distinct audiences. Adjust vocabulary, energy level, sentence length, and cultural reference accordingly. The same message should sound different across those three channels.

## Caption & Messaging Direction
 
### What Captions Are For
Captions are not announcements. They are the first sentence of a conversation with someone who doesn't know us yet. They should offer something — a thought, a question, a feeling — before they ask for anything.
 
### Themes That Land
Captions should reach toward themes that matter to real people in real life:
- **Personal growth** — becoming, learning, changing
- **Rest and balance** — permission to breathe; acknowledging the weight of modern pace
- **Cultural exploration** — wonder, curiosity, discovery
- **Authentic community** — real friendship, not performance; belonging that costs something honest
### Messaging Tone: Sophisticated, Not Safe
Generic invitations fall flat with the audience we're trying to reach. The tone should feel like a smart, grounded friend — not a flyer. Language should address real anxieties (loneliness, work-life tension, financial strain, the search for meaning) without being heavy-handed or preachy.
 
**Instead of:** *"Join us for worship this Sunday!"*
**Try:** *"Find a moment of peace and connection in a community that understands the demands of modern life."*
 
**Instead of:** *"We'd love to see you this weekend."*
**Try:** *"Explore timeless wisdom for a more balanced life this Sunday."*
 
### Content Series Framing
Posts and series should feel like **discussions and insights, not sermons.** Draw on themes that resonate with intellectually curious people:
- Work-life balance and overcoming burnout
- Mindful parenting and navigating modern family life
- Loneliness and the search for real community
- Financial peace and purpose
- Finding meaning in the middle of busy
These can be framed as: *"Finding Your Purpose: Lessons from [Topic]"* or *"Navigating Modern Parenthood: Community & Support"* — approachable, value-forward, non-preachy.

## Call-to-Action (CTA) Guidance
 
Generic CTAs ("Join us for worship") create friction for anyone who isn't already committed. The goal is **low-barrier, curiosity-first invitations** that allow people to engage at their own pace.
 
### CTA Principles
- **Invite exploration, not obligation.** The person we're reaching is skeptical. Give them an easy first step.
- **Offer value before asking for presence.** Link to content, not just a service.
- **Diversify.** Not every post should end with "Come this Sunday."
### CTA Examples That Work
- *"DM us your biggest question about faith."*
- *"Visit our website to learn more about our community groups."*
- *"Listen to last week's talk on [relevant topic] — link in bio."*
- *"RSVP for our next community event."*
- *"What's one thing you're holding onto right now? Drop it below."*
### CTAs to Retire
- "Join us for worship" as a default closer
- "We'd love to see you this Sunday" (vague, low-value)
- Any CTA that assumes the person is already comfortable showing up

## Brand Character in Words
 
These seven traits (from the brand guide) describe how the writing should *feel*. Use them as a gut-check before publishing anything.
 
| Trait | What It Sounds Like in Writing |
|---|---|
| **Simplicity** | One clear idea per post. Short sentences. Nothing extra. |
| **Authenticity** | Real language, honest observations, no performance. |
| **Empathy** | Meets people where they are. Doesn't assume everyone is fine. |
| **Joy** | Delight is allowed. Celebration is not fluff — it's theology. |
| **Creativity** | Unexpected angles. Fresh phrasing. Avoids the obvious frame. |
| **Excellence** | Precise word choices. Nothing sloppy or half-formed. |
| **Curiosity** | Asks good questions. Sits with tension rather than resolving it too fast. |

## Core Value Tension Pairs — Voice Application
 
The brand guide defines values as tension pairs. Each one has a direct implication for how content is written.
 
| We Sound Like → | We Don't Sound Like → |
|---|---|
| **Simple** — clean, clear, one thing at a time | **Overdone** — cluttered with adjectives, too much information |
| **Generous** — gives freely, assumes the best | **Obligatory** — guilt-based, "you should," transactional |
| **Curious** — asks, wonders, explores openly | **Certain** — knows all the answers, no room for questions |
| **Celebratory** — names wins, marks moments | **Critical** — focuses on what's wrong or what people lack |
| **Innovative** — finds a fresh angle on familiar truth | **Cliché** — leans on stock church phrases and tired metaphors |
| **Initiative-taking** — moves first, invites boldly | **Timid** — hedging, vague, non-committal |
| **Inviting** — written for the person who hasn't shown up yet | **Exclusive** — insider language, assumes familiarity |

## Audience Voices
 
### Main Brand (Adults)
**Tone:** Warm, grounded, curious, honest. Speaks to real life without being heavy-handed. Comfortable with complexity. Doesn't have all the answers and doesn't pretend to.
 
**Register:** Conversational prose. Short to medium sentences. Rhetorical questions work well. Plain language with theological depth underneath — not on the surface.
 
**What it sounds like in real Stone Creek writing:**
> *"Walking into a new church — or even stepping into a new season — can feel overwhelming. That's why we created this guide: to help you navigate everything happening this semester and feel at home."*
> — Winter Guide
 
> *"Transformation isn't just something that happens to us — it's something God does through us. And that kind of change isn't a single moment. It's a journey. A process. A way of life."*
> — Winter Guide
 
> *"At Stonecreek, we believe this kind of community doesn't just happen. It's intentionally practiced."*
> — Winter Guide
 
**Notice:** Short declarative sentences after a longer setup. Dashes used for rhythm. "We" language. No jargon. Grounded in the concrete ("this semester," "feel at home") before moving to the transcendent.
 
---
 
### Kids Brand
**Tone:** High-energy, playful, adventurous. Language is bold and short. Exclamation points are earned, not reflexive. Think: you're talking to a child who's genuinely excited — match their energy, lead with the fun, point to the wonder.
 
**Register:** Very short sentences. Active verbs. Big ideas made simple. The brand phrase "Let the Adventure Begin" captures the feel exactly.
 
**What it sounds like:**
> Direct, active, sensory. "Come see." "Find out." "Something big is happening." Questions that kids actually ask. Not "We invite you to explore your faith journey" — more like "Ready for this?"
 
---
 
### Youth (YTH) Brand
**Tone:** Direct, current, honest, zero-BS. Doesn't talk down. Meets teenagers at their actual intelligence level. Doesn't over-explain. Can hold tension and doubt without flinching.
 
**Register:** Short. Punchy. Culturally aware without trying too hard. Can be edgy in concept, not in content. The Electric Lime / Midnight Black visual palette tells you the energy: bold contrast, no softening.
 
**What it sounds like:**
> Statements that land without setup. Questions that don't have easy answers. Language that respects that the reader is smart and has seen a lot.

## The Tagline: *Go Together*
 
**"Go Together"** is not a sign-off. It is a theological statement about the kind of movement Stone Creek is — communal, directional, and active. Use it when content is specifically about:
- Community, belonging, or shared life
- Movement, journey, or next steps
- The church as a people, not just a place
**Don't** use it as a generic closer the way you'd use "Blessings" or "See you Sunday." That dilutes it.
 
**Related language in real Stone Creek copy:**
> *"gather. pray. serve."* (Winter Guide structural refrain)
> *"Leave it better than you found it."*
> *"Make our blocks better. Make our schools better. Make our workplaces better. Make our city better."*
 
These all carry the same DNA as "Go Together" — movement, community, purpose, place.

## Language That Is and Isn't Stone Creek
 
### Words and Phrases That Fit
- transformation / transforming
- together / community / neighborhood
- journey / process / way of life
- gather / serve / pray
- real / honest / everyday
- belonging / at home / welcome
- curious / questions / wonder
- practical / tangible
- hope / healing / renewal
### Words and Phrases to Avoid
- "missional community" (unless explained — insider jargon)
- "unchurched" / "unsaved" / "lost" (othering language)
- "join us for worship" / "join us for a powerful time of worship" (overused, vague, low-value CTA)
- "we'd love to see you this Sunday" (generic, no value offered)
- "fellowship" used generically
- "blessed and highly favored" as filler
- "we want to invite you to" (passive; just invite them)
- any phrase that makes the church sound like a product to be consumed
- guilt-based framing: "you should be here," "don't miss out"

## Voice in Practice: Before & After
 
**Announcing a new series:**
> ❌ "We are excited to invite you to join us as we kick off our brand new sermon series this Sunday!"
> ✅ "New series starts Sunday. It's going to ask some questions you've probably been sitting with for a while."
 
**Promoting community groups:**
> ❌ "Don't do life alone — join a Neighborhood Gathering today!"
> ✅ "Real friendships don't just happen. They're built. Neighborhood Gatherings are where that starts."
*(cf. "At Stonecreek, we believe this kind of community doesn't just happen. It's intentionally practiced." — Winter Guide)*
 
**Sharing a sermon moment:**
> ❌ "Such an amazing word from Pastor Ricky this Sunday! So blessed!"
> ✅ "This one landed: 'Patience allows you to celebrate small victories while you're waiting on the big one.'"
 
**Service opportunity:**
> ❌ "We are looking for volunteers to serve in our children's ministry this semester!"
> ✅ "You were made on purpose — for a purpose. If that's feeling abstract, serving is how it gets concrete."
*(cf. "You were made on purpose — for a purpose." — Winter Guide)*

## Platform-Specific Content Guidance
 
### Instagram
Instagram is a **visual-first, trend-driven platform.** Static graphics with generic text underperform. The audience here (especially the "Sophisticated Sarah" persona) expects modern aesthetics, diverse representation, and content that feels native to the platform.
 
**Reels & Stories — Priority Format**
Short-form video consistently outperforms static posts. Reels and Stories should:
- Showcase the **experience** of Stone Creek — not just service invitations, but what it actually feels like to be part of this community
- Feature **diverse faces, real moments, and behind-the-scenes glimpses** — kids' activities, community outreach, sermon prep, candid connection
- Use **trending audio and text overlays** that feel platform-native, not produced
- Be short, dynamic, and visually appealing — not repurposed announcement graphics
**Static Posts**
Reserve for high-quality visual content (quote graphics, event announcements with strong design). Generic graphics without a compelling hook will underperform — do not default to them.
 
### Facebook
Facebook allows for slightly more depth. Posts can carry a short paragraph — but still lead with something human and interesting before the information. Content series work well here as discussion-starters rather than announcements.
 
### Both Platforms
Do not post the same content identically across both platforms without adapting for format. Consistency in *theme* is good. Consistency in *exact format and copy* misses what each platform does well.

## Writing Mechanics
 
**Sentence length:** Mix short punches with longer builds. The Winter Guide does this consistently — a longer setup sentence, then a short declarative sentence that lands the point. *"It's a journey. A process. A way of life."*
 
**Punctuation for rhythm:** Em dashes (—) are used heavily in real Stone Creek copy to create rhythm and insert a secondary beat into a sentence. Use them. Don't overuse commas where a period would land harder.
 
**Capitalization:** The brand itself writes "stonecreek" in the wordmark (lowercase), but uses "Stonecreek" as a proper noun in prose. Follow that pattern in copy.
 
**Questions:** Stone Creek copy asks a lot of questions — especially to open a thought. "What do you pray about when you're caught in between?" (Prayer Guide). This is on-brand. Questions signal curiosity, invite reflection, and resist the certainty-vs-curiosity trap.
 
**Fragments:** Used intentionally for emphasis and rhythm. *"It's a journey. A process. A way of life."* These are not errors — they're tools.
 
**Scripture references:** When used, they appear as natural anchors, not proof-texts. "James 3:17–18" at the end of a section, not bolded at the top as an opener. Scripture informs the thought — it doesn't replace the writing.

## At-a-Glance Voice Check
 
Before posting, run the copy through these questions:
 
1. **Would a thoughtful person actually say this out loud?** If not, rewrite it.
2. **Does it invite or obligate?** Obligation is off-brand.
3. **Is there anything only an insider would understand?** If yes, either explain it or cut it.
4. **Is it simple enough?** One idea, clearly stated. Nothing extra.
5. **Does it serve the mission?** Does it point — even indirectly — toward transformation, belonging, or invitation?
6. **Would "Sophisticated Sarah" find this relevant to her actual life?** Or does it sound like church-world content she'd scroll past?
7. **Does the CTA lower the barrier or raise it?** If someone who's never been to Stone Creek sees this, is their next step obvious and easy?
8. **Is the format right for the platform?** A Reel should feel like a Reel. A caption should feel like a caption. Not a bulletin insert.
